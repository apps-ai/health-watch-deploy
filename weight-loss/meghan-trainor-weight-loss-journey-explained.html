Meghan Trainor's Weight Loss Journey Explained

Meghan Trainor has spoken candidly about her postpartum health journey, the changes she made, and how motherhood shaped her approach to wellness. Here's what she shared.

Sam Okonkwo
Reviewed by Alex Chen, MS, RD
July 13, 20266 min read

Introduction: Meghan Trainor's Health Transformation

Meghan Trainor — the Grammy-winning singer known for All About That Bass — has built a career in part around challenging narrow beauty standards. Her foray into public discussions about weight loss came in a notably personal context: the postpartum period following the birth of her children.

Trainor's story stands apart from many celebrity weight loss narratives because it was rooted in the specific physical and emotional realities of new parenthood — a context many of her fans share. Her openness about the challenges and her approach made her transformation particularly relatable.

What Has Meghan Trainor Said About Her Weight Loss?

Trainor has spoken about her wellness journey across interviews and social media, with consistent themes:

  • Motherhood was the catalyst: She has repeatedly referenced becoming a mother as the turning point that motivated her to focus more deliberately on her health and energy levels.
  • It was gradual, not dramatic: Trainor has emphasized that her approach was about sustainable lifestyle adjustments rather than rapid transformation.
  • She was specific about working with professionals: She has mentioned working with trainers and making intentional changes to her diet with guidance.
  • She has spoken about GLP-1 medication use: In public interviews, Trainor acknowledged using a weight loss medication as part of her approach — being candid in an industry where such transparency is uncommon.

Her account is notable for combining honesty about professional support and medical tools with an authentic, non-shaming narrative about her motivations.

What Diet or Fitness Changes Did She Describe?

Based on Trainor's public statements, her lifestyle adjustments included multiple components:

Exercise:

  • Incorporating more structured physical activity, including working with a personal trainer
  • Pilates and strength training have been referenced in her wellness discussions
  • Focusing on movement that she found enjoyable and sustainable rather than punishing regimens

Nutrition:

  • Shifting toward more intentional eating, with an emphasis on food quality
  • Reducing processed and high-sugar foods
  • She has not promoted any specific named diet protocol publicly

Medication:

  • Trainor has acknowledged using a weight loss medication, framing it as a tool to support — not replace — her lifestyle changes
  • Her candor about this aligns with growing public acceptance that medical weight management is a legitimate clinical option

How Did Motherhood Influence Her Approach?

The postpartum context of Trainor's journey shaped her approach in important ways that she has articulated:

Energy as a primary motivator: Rather than focusing on a target weight, she has spoken about wanting more energy to keep up with her children — a goal that feels fundamentally different from appearance-based motivation.

Time constraints: New parenthood is physically demanding and time-poor. Trainor has spoken about finding realistic, efficient approaches that fit into a parent's schedule rather than idealized wellness regimens.

Postpartum body acceptance: She has been thoughtful about the distinction between pursuing health and being at war with her postpartum body — acknowledging that the body changes after childbirth are normal and deserving of respect.

Shared journey with her husband: Trainor has spoken about her husband Daryl Sabara participating in wellness changes alongside her — an accountability dynamic that research supports as effective for adherence.

Healthy Takeaways: Expert Insights on Postpartum Wellness

The postpartum period is a clinically distinct context for weight management, and Trainor's approach reflects several evidence-based principles:

1. Postpartum weight loss timelines vary widely The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) recommends against aggressive weight loss in the first 6–8 weeks postpartum. After that, a gradual approach — aiming for no more than 0.5 kg (1 lb) per week — is recommended to preserve energy for breastfeeding (if applicable) and recovery.

2. Resistance training preserves muscle postpartum Incorporating strength training in the postpartum period helps rebuild core strength, improve pelvic floor function, and support metabolic health. Pilates, in particular, has good evidence for postpartum core rehabilitation.

3. Sleep deprivation complicates weight loss New parents are chronically sleep-deprived, and sleep deprivation significantly elevates hunger hormones (ghrelin) and reduces satiety signals (leptin). This is not a willpower issue — it's physiology. Realistic expectations and a non-punitive approach matter enormously in this context.

4. Social support and shared goals improve outcomes Evidence consistently shows that having a partner or peer pursuing similar health goals significantly improves long-term adherence. Trainor's references to her husband joining her wellness journey align with this research.

5. GLP-1 medications and breastfeeding It's worth noting that GLP-1 receptor agonist medications are generally not recommended during breastfeeding due to limited safety data. Anyone considering weight management medication in the postpartum period should discuss timing carefully with their obstetrician or physician.

Conclusion: What We Can Learn

Meghan Trainor's postpartum wellness journey offers a grounded template for new parents navigating similar challenges. The core lessons are:

  • Motivation rooted in energy and presence — wanting to keep up with your children — is more sustainable than aesthetically-driven goals
  • Gradual, professional-supported approaches consistently outperform rapid transformations
  • Honesty about all tools used, including medication, helps normalize medical weight management and reduces stigma
  • The postpartum body deserves patience and respect — recovery is not linear, and health progress should be measured by how you feel, not just how you look
