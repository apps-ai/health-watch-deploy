f:T6e2,<p>To lose body fat, you must consume fewer calories than your body burns—a state known as a calorie deficit. While the math is simple, the biology of human metabolism and appetite is complex.</p>
<h2>Calculating Your Daily Energy Expenditure</h2>
<p>Your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) consists of four components:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR)</strong>: Energy burned at rest to keep you alive (60-70% of TDEE).</li>
<li><strong>Thermic Effect of Food (TEF)</strong>: Energy used to digest nutrients (10% of TDEE).</li>
<li><strong>Exercise Activity Thermogenesis (EAT)</strong>: Energy burned during structured workouts (5-10% of TDEE).</li>
<li><strong>Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis (NEAT)</strong>: Energy burned during daily movement like walking, typing, and fidgeting (15-30% of TDEE).</li>
</ol>
<p>To find your TDEE, use an online calculator using the Mifflin-St Jeor equation, then subtract <strong>300 to 500 calories</strong> to set your daily target.</p>
<h2>Strategies for a Sustainable Deficit</h2>
<p>Starving yourself is a recipe for failure. It triggers intense hunger and causes muscle loss. Instead, use these strategies:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Prioritize Protein</strong>: Protein has a high thermic effect (digesting it burns calories) and increases satiety hormones. Aim for 1.6–2.2g of protein per kg of body weight.</li>
<li><strong>Eat Volume Foods</strong>: Choose high-volume, low-calorie foods (like leafy greens, broccoli, berries, and cucumber) to physically fill your stomach.</li>
<li><strong>Focus on NEAT</strong>: Keep daily step counts high (aim for 8,000–10,000 steps). When you eat less, your body naturally tries to move less to conserve energy; active steps counteract this.</li>
