Keto Diet Plan: A Step-by-Step Starter Guide

Everything you need to start a keto diet plan: what to eat, carb limits, a 7-day meal plan, how long it takes to work, and how to beat keto flu.

alex-chen
Reviewed by sam-okonkwo, MD, MPH
July 12, 20269 min read

The ketogenic diet has moved from a niche medical therapy to one of the most searched dietary approaches in the world. And for good reason — when implemented correctly, it can produce rapid weight loss, improved energy stability, and significant metabolic benefits. The challenge for most beginners is that keto requires more precision than other diets, especially at the start.

What Is a Keto Diet Plan?

A ketogenic diet plan is a very low-carbohydrate, high-fat eating protocol designed to shift your body into a metabolic state called ketosis. In ketosis, your liver converts fat (both dietary and stored body fat) into molecules called ketone bodies, which become your body's primary fuel source in place of glucose.

This metabolic shift is the key mechanism behind keto's benefits: when glucose is unavailable, the body becomes highly efficient at burning fat. The result is accelerated fat loss, reduced hunger, and for many people, greater mental clarity.

How Many Carbs Can You Eat on Keto?

Carbohydrate intake is the critical lever in ketogenic eating. Standard keto guidelines:

  • Total carbs: Under 50 grams per day
  • Net carbs (total carbs minus fiber): Under 20–30 grams per day for most people to achieve ketosis
  • Fat: Approximately 65–75% of total calories
  • Protein: 20–30% of total calories (too much protein can be converted to glucose through gluconeogenesis)
  • Carbohydrates: 5–10% of total calories

Individual thresholds vary. Some people achieve ketosis at 50g net carbs; others need to stay under 20g. A blood or urine ketone meter can confirm whether you're in ketosis.

What Foods Are on a Keto Diet Plan?

Eat freely:

  • Fats: Avocado, olive oil, butter, ghee, coconut oil, MCT oil
  • Proteins: Eggs, fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines), beef, pork, chicken thighs (with skin)
  • Dairy: Cheese, heavy cream, full-fat Greek yogurt (in moderation)
  • Low-carb vegetables: Leafy greens, spinach, zucchini, cauliflower, broccoli, peppers, asparagus
  • Nuts & seeds: Macadamia nuts, pecans, walnuts, chia seeds, hemp seeds

Avoid:

  • All grains (bread, pasta, rice, oats)
  • Sugar and sweetened beverages
  • Most fruits (except small portions of berries)
  • Starchy vegetables (potatoes, corn, sweet potatoes, carrots in excess)
  • Legumes (beans, lentils, chickpeas)
  • Most condiments and sauces (often contain hidden sugars)

How Long Does Keto Take to Work?

Keto has a distinct timeline of effects:

  • Days 1–3: Glycogen depletion. Your body burns through stored carbohydrates. You may feel tired or mentally foggy.
  • Days 3–7: Ketosis begins. Some people enter ketosis within 3 days; others take up to a week. Early signs include reduced appetite and a slightly fruity breath odor (from acetone).
  • Week 2–4: "Keto-adapted" state begins to develop. Energy stabilizes, mental clarity often improves, and fat burning accelerates.
  • Months 2–3+: Full fat adaptation. Athletic performance, hunger regulation, and energy levels typically normalize.

Most people notice measurable weight loss in the first 1–2 weeks, though this includes significant water weight loss as glycogen stores are depleted (glycogen holds approximately 3–4 grams of water per gram).

What Is Keto Flu and How to Avoid It?

Keto flu is a cluster of symptoms that typically appears during the first 3–7 days of starting keto. Symptoms include:

  • Headache
  • Fatigue and brain fog
  • Irritability
  • Muscle cramps
  • Nausea
  • Difficulty sleeping

Keto flu is caused primarily by electrolyte loss. As glycogen is depleted and insulin drops, the kidneys excrete sodium, which in turn causes losses of potassium and magnesium. Preventing it is straightforward:

  1. Salt your food liberally or add sodium through broth
  2. Take magnesium glycinate (200–400 mg/day)
  3. Eat potassium-rich keto foods: avocado, leafy greens, salmon
  4. Stay very well hydrated
  5. Transition gradually if needed — reduce carbs over 1–2 weeks rather than cutting all at once

Who Should Not Try Keto?

While keto is safe for most healthy adults, it's not appropriate for everyone:

  • Type 1 diabetics: At risk for diabetic ketoacidosis without medical supervision
  • Pregnant or breastfeeding women: Nutritional needs are altered; consult a physician
  • People with pancreatitis or liver disease: High fat intake may worsen these conditions
  • Those on certain medications: Insulin and blood pressure medications may need dosage adjustment as keto improves metabolic markers

Always consult a healthcare provider before starting keto if you have an existing medical condition.

Starting a keto diet plan is a significant metabolic shift that rewards patience and preparation. Those who succeed long-term are typically the ones who invest time in understanding their macros, stocking a keto-friendly pantry, and proactively managing electrolytes. With the right foundation, keto can be a powerful tool for weight management and metabolic health.
