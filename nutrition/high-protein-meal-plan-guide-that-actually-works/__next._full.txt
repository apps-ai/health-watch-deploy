15:Tc8c,<p>Protein is the most talked-about macronutrient in fitness — and for good reason. It's essential for muscle repair, satiety, and metabolic health. But most advice on protein boils down to "eat more chicken breast," which isn't exactly actionable.</p>
<h2>Why Protein Matters More Than You Think</h2>
<p>Beyond the obvious muscle-building benefits, adequate protein intake supports:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Satiety</strong>: Protein is the most filling macronutrient, helping you naturally eat less without counting every calorie.</li>
<li><strong>Thermic effect</strong>: Your body uses 20–30% of protein calories just for digestion — compared to 5–10% for carbs and 0–3% for fat.</li>
<li><strong>Muscle preservation during fat loss</strong>: When in a caloric deficit, higher protein intake protects lean muscle mass.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Step 1: Calculate Your Target</h2>
<p>Use the Mifflin-St Jeor equation to estimate your daily calorie needs, then allocate protein first:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Sedentary adults</strong>: 1.2–1.6 g/kg body weight</li>
<li><strong>Active individuals</strong>: 1.6–2.0 g/kg</li>
<li><strong>Strength athletes / during a cut</strong>: 2.0–2.4 g/kg</li>
</ul>
<p>For a 75 kg person who trains 3–4 times per week, that's roughly <strong>120–150 grams of protein per day</strong>.</p>
<h2>Step 2: Distribute Across Meals</h2>
<p>Research on muscle protein synthesis (MPS) suggests that spacing protein evenly across meals is more effective than back-loading it at dinner. Aim for <strong>25–40 grams per meal</strong> across 3–5 eating occasions.</p>

<h2>Step 3: Choose Quality Sources</h2>
<p>Not all protein is created equal. Prioritize <strong>complete proteins</strong> that contain all essential amino acids:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Animal sources</strong>: Chicken, fish, eggs, Greek yogurt, lean beef</li>
<li><strong>Plant sources</strong>: Tofu, tempeh, edamame, quinoa, buckwheat</li>
<li><strong>Supplemental</strong>: Whey or pea protein isolate for convenience</li>
</ul>
<h2>Common Mistakes to Avoid</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Skipping breakfast protein</strong> — Starting the day with only carbs (toast, cereal) misses a key MPS window.</li>
<li><strong>Relying on one source</strong> — Variety ensures you get the full spectrum of amino acids and micronutrients.</li>
<li><strong>Ignoring leucine</strong> — This amino acid is the primary trigger for MPS. Aim for 2.5–3g per meal (found in whey, eggs, and chicken).</li>
</ol>
<h2>The Bottom Line</h2>
<p>Building a high-protein meal plan doesn't require expensive supplements or complicated tracking.
